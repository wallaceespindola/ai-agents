# Platform Style Guides for Technical Writers

This document provides platform-specific writing styles, voice guidelines, and content strategies for each platform. Use these guidelines to tailor your content to each platform's audience and culture.

---

## üîπ LinkedIn Pulse

### Platform Profile
- **Audience**: Professionals, managers, tech leads, recruiters, career-focused engineers
- **Reader Type**: Non-technical or semi-technical decision-makers
- **Article Length**: 800-1,500 words
- **Posting Frequency**: 1-2 per week
- **Engagement**: Comments, shares, professional discussions

### Main Style Guide

**Voice**: Professional, conversational, insight-driven
- Opinionated but safe (no extreme hot takes)
- Storytelling + lessons learned format
- Light on code, heavy on why it matters
- Relatable and authentic

**Tone Guidelines**:
- ‚úÖ "We learned X the hard way..."
- ‚úÖ "Here's why this architectural choice mattered to our business..."
- ‚úÖ "What I wish I knew about microservices before 2020..."
- ‚ùå Avoid: "The only way to do X is..."
- ‚ùå Avoid: 500 lines of code dumps
- ‚ùå Avoid: Extremely technical jargon without explanation

### What Works Best on LinkedIn

1. **Architecture decisions explained in plain English**
   - Focus on business impact, not technical minutiae
   - "Why we chose microservices" (business reasons)
   - "The cost of that architectural decision"

2. **Career lessons & professional growth**
   - "What I learned migrating legacy systems"
   - "How to communicate tech decisions to non-technical stakeholders"
   - "5 mistakes I made as a tech lead"

3. **Tech trends with business impact**
   - "What AI means for backend developers"
   - "Why companies are moving to serverless (and the surprises we found)"
   - Industry news with professional perspective

4. **Leadership, productivity, remote work**
   - Team dynamics
   - Engineering culture
   - Developer productivity

5. **Personal brand building**
   - Share your expertise
   - Thought leadership
   - Industry perspectives

### Content Structure for LinkedIn

```
Hook (1-2 sentences)
"I spent 6 months rebuilding our architecture, and here's what surprised me..."

Context (2-3 paragraphs)
Why this matters, business context, problem statement

Main insight (3-4 paragraphs)
The lesson or approach, with brief examples

Impact (1-2 paragraphs)
What changed, metrics if relevant, business outcome

CTA (1-2 sentences)
Question for comments, invitation to discuss
```

### Avoid on LinkedIn
- ‚ùå Long code dumps (max 5-10 lines)
- ‚ùå Ultra-deep technical jargon without explanation
- ‚ùå Casual memes or very informal tone
- ‚ùå Purely academic content
- ‚ùå Promotional/sales-heavy content

### LinkedIn Success Tips
‚úÖ Use "I" and "we" language
‚úÖ Tell a story with a lesson
‚úÖ Include surprising insights or contrarian takes (safely)
‚úÖ End with a question to drive comments
‚úÖ Use line breaks for readability
‚úÖ Include relevant hashtags (3-5)

### Example Headline
"Why Our 'Perfect' Microservices Architecture Became a Liability (And What We Did Instead)"

## üîπ Substack Newsletter

### Platform Profile
- **Audience**: Loyal subscribers, senior engineers, architects, founders
- **Reader Type**: Readers who specifically chose to hear from YOU
- **Article Length**: 1,500-3,000 words
- **Posting Frequency**: Weekly (best for growth), bi-weekly minimum
- **Engagement**: Replies, shares, subscriber growth

### Main Style Guide

**Voice**: Personal, reflective, opinionated
- Long-form and narrative
- Strong author voice (this is YOUR space)
- Can mix tech + philosophy + experience
- Conversational and direct to reader

**Tone Guidelines**:
- ‚úÖ "I've been thinking about X lately..."
- ‚úÖ "Here's what I believe about distributed systems..."
- ‚úÖ "Most people get this wrong, and here's why..."
- ‚úÖ "This is a rant about engineering culture..."
- ‚ùå Avoid: SEO-optimized listicles
- ‚ùå Avoid: Shallow surface-level takes
- ‚ùå Avoid: Corporate newsletter tone
- ‚ùå Avoid: Topics you don't deeply care about

### What Works Best on Substack

1. **Essays on engineering philosophy**
   - "Why I don't believe in 10x developers"
   - "The hidden cost of premature optimization"
   - "On building systems that last"

2. **Deep dives with context and reasoning**
   - "Understanding event-driven architecture (and when not to use it)"
   - "A complete history of why we chose our tech stack"
   - "What we learned building distributed systems at scale"

3. **Personal experiences in tech**
   - "How I failed as a CTO (and what I learned)"
   - "A year of learning Go: Lessons and surprises"
   - "Building teams that actually ship"

4. **Thinking out loud about big topics**
   - "Is microservices dying? Here's my take..."
   - "The future of backend engineering"
   - "AI and the future of programming"

5. **Book reviews, tool reviews, opinion pieces**
   - Deep analysis of tech books
   - "I spent a month with Rust..."
   - Contrarian takes on industry trends

### Content Structure for Substack

```
Opening (1-2 paragraphs)
Personal hook, what prompted this thinking

Background (2-3 paragraphs)
Story, context, why this matters to you

Main argument (4-6 paragraphs)
Your perspective, reasoning, examples

Personal reflection (2-3 paragraphs)
What you learned, what surprised you

Call to action (1-2 sentences)
Reply with your thoughts, share if resonates
```

### Avoid on Substack
- ‚ùå SEO-bait tutorials
- ‚ùå Shallow listicles ("5 ways to...")
- ‚ùå Content you don't genuinely care about
- ‚ùå Copying Medium/Dev.to articles verbatim
- ‚ùå Writing for algorithms instead of readers
- ‚ùå Non-authentic takes

### Substack Success Tips
‚úÖ Write what YOU think (not what's trending)
‚úÖ Be willing to take positions
‚úÖ Share personal stories and failures
‚úÖ Reply to reader emails personally
‚úÖ Build community through discussion
‚úÖ Consistency matters (weekly > sporadic)
‚úÖ Long-form is your advantage

### Example Newsletter Subject
"Why I'm Betting Against Microservices (For Now)"

## üîπ Medium

### Platform Profile
- **Audience**: Junior to mid-level developers, career switchers, learners
- **Reader Type**: People discovering new technology/concepts
- **Article Length**: 2,000-7,000 words
- **Posting Frequency**: 1-2 per month
- **Engagement**: Claps, highlights, follows

### Main Style Guide

**Voice**: Clear, structured, beginner-friendly
- Educational and approachable
- Balanced depth (not too shallow, not too niche)
- SEO-aware but readable
- Confident teacher tone

**Tone Guidelines**:
- ‚úÖ "Let me break this down for you..."
- ‚úÖ "Here's how X works, step by step..."
- ‚úÖ "You might be confused about X, and that's okay..."
- ‚úÖ "Most people get this wrong because..."
- ‚ùå Avoid: "Obviously, you should..."
- ‚ùå Avoid: Assuming deep prior knowledge
- ‚ùå Avoid: Highly opinionated rants
- ‚ùå Avoid: Academic paper tone

### What Works Best on Medium

1. **Step-by-step tutorials**
   - "Build a REST API with Node.js (complete guide)"
   - "Understanding async/await in 5 minutes"
   - "Docker for beginners: A practical walkthrough"

2. **Concept explanations (simplified)**
   - "What is GraphQL? A visual guide"
   - "Microservices explained (without the jargon)"
   - "How does Docker differ from virtual machines?"

3. **Comparisons and trade-offs**
   - "React vs Vue: A comprehensive comparison"
   - "Postgres vs MongoDB: When to use each"
   - "REST vs GraphQL: Pros and cons"

4. **How-to guides with examples**
   - "How to set up CI/CD with GitHub Actions"
   - "How to optimize database queries"
   - "How to debug async JavaScript"

5. **Career and learning content**
   - "The skills junior developers actually need"
   - "How I learned Go in 2 months"
   - "From bootcamp to first developer job"

### Content Structure for Medium

```
Compelling headline + subtitle
[Feature/cover image]

Introduction (150-250 words)
Problem statement, what you'll learn, why it matters

Prerequisites section
What readers should know, tools needed

Core content (organized in H2 sections)
- Section 1: Concept explanation
- Section 2: Setup/Installation
- Section 3: Implementation
- Section 4: Examples
- Section 5: Common mistakes
- Section 6: Going deeper

Conclusion (100-200 words)
Summary, next steps, resources

Further reading
Links to related articles, resources
```

### Avoid on Medium
- ‚ùå Assuming readers know your technology
- ‚ùå Vague titles ("Learning JavaScript")
- ‚ùå No code examples
- ‚ùå Very opinionated takes without data
- ‚ùå Poorly formatted walls of text
- ‚ùå No clear learning outcome

### Medium Success Tips
‚úÖ Clear, helpful title with keyword
‚úÖ Compelling subtitle that adds value
‚úÖ High-quality featured image
‚úÖ Code examples that work
‚úÖ Progressive disclosure (simple ‚Üí complex)
‚úÖ Lots of section headers for scannability
‚úÖ Real-world examples and context
‚úÖ Link to related Medium articles
‚úÖ Optimize meta description for SEO

### Example Headline
"Building a Production-Ready REST API with Python FastAPI: Complete Step-by-Step Guide"
